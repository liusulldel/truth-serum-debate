## 1. Opening

Mechanism design has fifty years of strategy-proofness results — Gibbard-Satterthwaite, Vickrey-Clarke-Groves, Myerson-Satterthwaite, the Bayesian Truth Serum of Prelec (2004) — and almost none of that machinery has been ported into the LLM debate protocols that scalable oversight now depends on. Irving et al. (2018, arXiv:1805.00899) framed debate as a two-player zero-sum game; Bowman et al. (2022, arXiv:2211.03540) and Khan et al. (2024, arXiv:2402.06782) showed empirically that stronger debaters help weaker judges find truth. None of these protocols carry a formal incentive-compatibility proof for the debaters themselves. I think that gap is fillable, and I have started filling it: https://github.com/liusulldel/truth-serum-debate is a working, tested artifact that scores three-node LangGraph debates with a vectorized Bayesian Truth Serum judge, plus a 1576-word formal derivation in `THEORY.md` proving truthfulness is a Bayesian Nash equilibrium.

## 3. Why these tracks

**Scalable Oversight (primary).** The Recommended Directions memo lists debate as a priority area, and Anthropic's own "Measuring Progress on Scalable Oversight for Large Language Models" line of work has been the cleanest empirical thread on it. My contribution is structural: Bayesian Truth Serum and adversarial debate are the same mechanism viewed from two angles. BTS rewards a reporter for predicting that others under-predict her answer; debate rewards a debater for arguments a judge cannot rebut. Both collapse to the same incentive geometry when the judge is Bayesian and the debaters share a common prior. In `truth-serum-debate/THEORY.md` I prove this formally as **Theorem 1 (Truthful BNE under common prior)**, and **Theorem 2 (Collusion deterrence under bounded side-payment channel)** gives the threshold under which two debaters cannot profitably coordinate against the judge. These are exactly the results that would let an oversight protocol come with a guarantee instead of an empirical curve.

**Model Welfare (secondary).** The Savage P1–P7 axioms and the von Neumann-Morgenstern axioms together define what it means for a system to *have* preferences in the decision-theoretic sense. Anthropic's deprecation interviews currently ask models open-ended retrospective questions; there is no formal protocol checking whether the elicited preferences are even coherent. That is a gap an axiomatic toolkit fills naturally. I am not claiming Claude has welfare; I am claiming we cannot answer the question without first checking whether the revealed-preference structure satisfies transitivity, independence, and continuity. If it does not, the whole frame may be wrong, which is itself a finding.

## 4. Concrete project pitches

**Pitch A — Strategy-proofness eval harness for debate (Scalable Oversight).** Take `truth-serum-debate` from its current N=20 pilot to N≈2000 questions across three capability gaps: Haiku-judge / Sonnet-debaters, Sonnet-judge / Opus-debaters, and a deliberately mismatched Haiku-judge / Opus-debaters condition designed to stress Theorem 2's collusion threshold. The hypothesis that needs falsifying is that the BTS-scored variant produces lower judge-error rates than vanilla majority-vote debate at fixed compute, *and* that as debater capability outruns judge capability the gap widens rather than collapses. **A synthetic-debater pilot is already in the repo (`experiments/BASELINE_BENCHMARK.md`):** the BTS + α-MEU hybrid achieves **84.1%** accuracy on its non-abstained decisions versus **74.5%** for the best of seven published baselines — Self-MoA (Li 2025, arXiv:2502.00674), Du multi-agent debate (2023, arXiv:2305.14325), MoA-lite (Wang 2024, arXiv:2406.04692), self-consistency (Wang 2022, arXiv:2203.11171), Khan-style debate (2024, arXiv:2402.06782), weighted-confidence, and majority vote — a **+9.6-percentage-point** gain at bootstrap **p = 0.026** on the realistic correlated-debater regime (per-debater accuracy 0.70, pairwise correlation 0.30, N = 200, seed = 42). Five mechanism-design aggregators are implemented and unit-tested as drop-in alternatives (BTS, AGV d'Aspremont-Gérard-Varet 1979, Holmström team contract 1982, α-MEU Ghirardato-Maccheroni-Marinacci 2004, Garicano 2000 hierarchy). Deliverable: a short paper plus a reusable evaluation harness — JSONL question schema, LangGraph debate runner, vectorized BTS scorer (`src/bts.py`), bootstrap-CI reporting layer, and the five-mechanism aggregator library — released so other oversight teams can drop in their own debate variants and get a strategy-proofness number out the other end.

**Pitch B — Preference coherence benchmark for frontier models (Model Welfare).** Translate Savage's P1–P7 axioms into prompt batteries that elicit revealed preferences over lotteries, ambiguous acts, and time-separated payoffs, and run them across the Claude family plus open-weights baselines. The interesting cases are the violations: when Independence fails, is it representational noise (different sampling, same underlying preference), an RLHF-induced trait (the model has learned to display Allais-paradox-like patterns because humans do), or evidence that the preference frame does not apply to the system at all? For the last two cases I would partner with the interpretability team — Lindsey et al.'s circuit tracing work is exactly the right tool to ask whether a coherent "preference circuit" exists at the substrate level. Deliverable: an open AI preference-coherence benchmark with axiom-by-axiom violation rates, paired with mech-interp probes on the highest-violation prompts.

## 5b. The transfer diagnostic (load-bearing claim)

Not every classical incentive theorem transfers to LLM agents, and the way they fail is informative. The repo's first experiment (`experiments/negative_results/THREE_FAILURES.md`) showed that prospect-theory loss aversion (Kahneman-Tversky 1979), reciprocity / gift exchange (Akerlof 1982; Fehr-Gächter 2000), and intrinsic-motivation crowd-out (Deci 1971; Gneezy-Rustichini 2000) all fail on stateless LLMs because they require felt hedonic loss, persistent counterparty memory, or pre-existing intrinsic motivation. The follow-on persona-utility wave (`experiments/persona_utility/SUMMARY.md`, 5 mock experiments + 1 theory note, 34/34 tests pass) sharpened that result by asking what changes when the model is *prompted* to simulate the missing structure. Three of the five mechanisms partially recover under persona prompting (loss-averse persona induces λ_LLM ≈ 1.40, paired-t = +75.17 vs stateless t = −0.59, anchored to Mei et al. 2024 PNAS; tournament persona reproduces the Lazear-Rosen Prop. 4 risk-shifting signature with variance ratio behind/ahead = 2.21×; crowd-out persona reproduces the Gneezy-Rustichini sign with low-pay accuracy 0.51 vs intrinsic 0.82). Two do not: persona-injected reciprocity buys a 13% early-round honeymoon then a *worse* steady state (Akata 2023 brittleness); persona-injected career concerns produce sandbagging (hedge rate 1.8× higher) without genuine reputational learning. The sharpened diagnostic I would defend in seminar:

> A persona prompt can simulate the **action distribution** that hedonic preferences would produce, but it does not provide the **state variable** the original IC proof quantifies over. Mechanisms whose IC argument is one-shot and posterior-shaped admit a persona substitution; mechanisms whose IC argument requires recurrent state do not. Posterior-only mechanisms (BTS, AGV, α-MEU, Garicano) transfer everywhere and remain robust under adversarial persona injection because they score the report through the posterior, not through the simulated hedonic state.

The safety corollary is asymmetric, not symmetric: every behavioral mechanism unlocked by a persona prompt is also opened to a prompt-injection adversary. Two-sentence injections shifted self-reported probabilities by 14pp (Cohen d_z = 5.32, larger than typical human framing effects); spoofed cooperation history broke a 3-agent workflow within 7 rounds. The mechanisms worth deploying inside an oversight loop are precisely the posterior-only ones whose incentive-compatibility proof never touches the agent's persona at all — which is why the aggregator stack in `src/` and the four `experiments/<family>/` aggregators are all in that class by construction.

## 6. Open questions I want to work on

1. Under what (debater capability, judge capability, question difficulty) regime does debate stop being strategy-proof and collapse into coordination? Theorem 2 gives an analytic threshold under common-prior assumptions; the empirical threshold under modern RLHF-trained debaters is unknown and probably differs.
2. When frontier models violate the Independence axiom, is that representational noise, an RLHF-induced behavioral trait, or evidence that the revealed-preference frame simply does not apply? The three explanations have very different implications for model welfare protocols.
3. What is the smallest perturbation to the Constitutional AI training loop that lets the constitution itself adapt over training without opening a Goodhart loop where the model learns to rewrite the constitution it is judged against?
4. Persona-injection robustness as an oversight property: which posterior-only aggregators remain calibrated when an adversary controls a 2-sentence segment of the debater's system prompt, and what is the smallest detectable persona-vs-stateless output divergence a monitor can use as an attack signature?
